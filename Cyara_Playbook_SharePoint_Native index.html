01 · Foundations

Role of the
Manager

At Cyara, managers are responsible for driving performance, developing people, and delivering results — while reinforcing a high-performance culture.

This is not a coordination role. It is a leadership and accountability role. Managers are the primary driver of performance, engagement, and culture within their teams.

🎯
Managers are expected to
  • Deliver team outcomes aligned to business priorities
  • Set clear expectations and ensure accountability for performance
  • Make fair, consistent, and evidence-based decisions
  • Develop individuals and build team capability over time
  • Reinforce behaviours that support winning outcomes and company values
  • Prioritise effectively and focus the team on what matters most
  • Remove obstacles and enable the team to deliver effectively
✅
What Success Looks Like
Strong Managers
  • Set clear, outcome-based goals
  • Hold individuals accountable for delivery
  • Make consistent, evidence-based decisions
  • Provide clear, timely feedback
  • Address underperformance early and directly
Their Teams
  • Clarity on expectations
  • Strong alignment to priorities
  • Consistent delivery of outcomes
  • High engagement and accountability

02 · Performance System

Goal Setting &
Accountability

All managers are accountable for setting the quality bar — not just approving goals. If we can't measure it, we can't fairly assess it.

🎯
The Standard
4–6
SMART goals per employee
100%
Goal weights must total
~1
Goal should meaningfully stretch
  • Every goal includes: metric + target + timeframe
  • Goals are outcome-based, not task lists
  • Goals clearly align to team and company priorities
  • Success is measurable and accountable
Your role as a manager: You are not approving goals. You are setting the quality bar. You are responsible for defining what "winning" looks like in your team.
🛠
When Reviewing Goals — Ask Consistently
  • "How will success be measured?"
  • "What outcome does this drive?"
  • "What would great look like?"
  • "Could we defend this at review?"
👉 If the answer isn't clear — the goal isn't ready.
⚠️ Send goals back if they…
  • Lack a metric, baseline, or target
  • Have no timeframe
  • Read like a task list
  • Don't link to team or company priorities
  • Cannot be objectively measured
⚖️
Evidence-Based Decision Making
  • Evidence-based, not opinion-based
  • Reflective of the full review period
  • Focused on impact, not activity
  • Aligned to role expectations
  • Clear, consistent, and defensible in calibration
⚠️
Biases to Actively Mitigate
Recency Bias
Review the full cycle — not just what's top of mind
Halo / Horns Effect
One strong or weak moment shouldn't define the whole view
Affinity Bias
Don't favour those who are most like you
Confirmation Bias
Look for evidence that challenges your assumptions too

03 · Managing People

Feedback, Coaching
& Difficult Conversations

Strong performance reviews and people conversations are decision-making moments — not just process steps.

🛠
What Good Reviews Look Like
Whole-period view
Not just recent performance — the full cycle
Specific evidence
Examples, not impressions or generalisations
Impact focus
Effect on team, work, and outcomes
Role alignment
Assessed against expectations, not peers
Balanced view
Both strengths and development areas
Clarity
Concise, well-structured feedback that stands alone
👉 One strong example is more valuable than multiple vague statements.
⚖️
Before Submitting — Ask Yourself
  • Is this evidence-based?
  • Is it balanced and fair?
  • Have I checked for bias?
  • Could I confidently explain this in calibration?
If not — refine before submitting. Your written review is what leadership uses to make decisions.
💬
Leading Performance Conversations

Strong managers lead with clarity, not comfort. They separate performance (facts) from potential (future), ground conversations in evidence and outcomes, and set clear expectations for next steps and development.

⚠️ Common Pitfalls
  • Overweighting recent performance
  • Using vague or generic feedback
  • Comparing employees to each other
  • Avoiding difficult performance calls
  • Confusing performance with promotion readiness
✅ Strong Approach
  • Anchor feedback to goals and evidence
  • Describe behaviours, not personality traits
  • Pause before submitting decisions
  • Write with calibration in mind
  • Review performance across the full cycle
Managers are accountable for being evidence gatherers, bias interrupters, and clear storytellers — articulating rationale that stands on its own.

04 · Culture & Business Impact

Recognition &
Winning Behaviours

Recognition is a core leadership behaviour at Cyara — not optional, and not just a nice-to-have. What gets recognised gets repeated.

🎯
The Standard
  • Use recognition consistently throughout the month — not in bursts
  • Provide specific, outcome-focused, behaviour-aligned recognition
  • Link recognition to company values and winning behaviours
  • Recognise both individual and cross-team contributions
  • Use the majority of your monthly Cynar allocation (200/month)

Closing the recognition gap is a key priority for 2026. Peer recognition is strong — manager-led recognition is not. This is on you.
🛠
What High-Quality Recognition Looks Like
Specific
Clearly describes what the person did — not just "great job"
Impact-Focused
Explains why it mattered — to the customer, team, or outcome
Values-Aligned
Links to company behaviours — not just effort
Timely
Given close to the moment — not weeks later
Visible
Shared so others can learn from and build on it
"Your proactive communication during the customer escalation kept everyone aligned and prevented delays. A great example of ownership and collaboration."
Behaviour → Impact → Value — this is the formula.
⚖️
Recognition vs Reward
Recognition
  • Ongoing and frequent
  • Reinforces behaviours and impact
  • Happens in real time via Bonusly
Reward
  • Structured: bonus, compensation, promotion
  • Based on sustained performance
  • Through formal performance processes
Recognition supports reward — but does not replace it.
⚠️ Common Pitfalls to Avoid
  • Generic praise ("Great job!") without specifics
  • Recognising effort without explaining impact
  • Delayed recognition — weeks after the moment
  • Inconsistent usage — all at month-end
  • Only recognising within your own team
